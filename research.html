My Research

I currently work at the intersection of outbreak modelling and viral evolution and genomics. I have developed a simulation framework that combines a pre-existing epidemiological model of rabies with an evolutionary model that allows me to generate synthetic genetic data similar to the data that my colleagues produce in the field. Using this framework I can explore questions regarding rabies evolution at different timescales, evaluate the tools we apply to genomic data and develop new methods.

Exploring how rabies evolves within the host

The first chapter of my PhD thesis (available as a preprint here!) uses this simulation framework to investigate whether some of the issues we have with phylogenetic analysis with rabies could be due to the virus' variable incubation period lengths. We implemented both a strict clock model of evolution and a per-generation model of evolution, representiing the two extreme possibilities of how mutational events could be distributed within a host. We found that at low substitution rates, these models behave functionally identically, as mutation is such a rare event that most within-host passages will not see and SNPs occur. However, a difference in behaviour was seen at substitution rates above 1 substitution per generation.
